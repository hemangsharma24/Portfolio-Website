01 The Problem

FNOL today is a data-entry problem
during someone's worst day.

Most carriers approach First Notice of Loss as a form to fill out. Agents are handed rigid templates exactly when policyholders are overwhelmed, distracted, and missing half the information. A few patterns kept showing up.

01

Agents expected to collect complete information upfront

Even when only partial details are available — the system asks for everything, even what the caller doesn't have yet.

02

Information arrives gradually — the system doesn't

Claims surface over hours, sometimes days. But the intake form treats it as a single, linear capture.

03

Manual entry across systems creates errors later

Agents copy data between tools. Small mistakes early multiply into bigger problems downstream.

04

Critical context isn't visible at intake

If a catastrophe event is active in the policyholder's area, the agent finds out later — if at all.

Each small gap at intake compounded into a larger problem in the claim's journey.

02 A Different Way to Design

Skipping Figma, on purpose.

Traditional handoffs hit the same wall: stakeholders interpret static screens differently and fill the gaps with their own assumptions. What gets approved isn't always what gets built.

So I tried something different. I designed and prototyped the entire workspace inside Claude — building a real, clickable experience in code rather than producing flat mockups. Three things changed because of that choice:

01 · Validate

Pressure-test the problem first.

I used Claude to challenge the framing before designing anything. The questions Claude pushed back with shaped what the workspace actually needed to solve.

02 · Build

Prototype directly in code.

Working states, real form behavior, AI assistant flows, conditional logic — not static screens. The output was something stakeholders could open in a browser and use.

03 · Iterate

Feedback as conversation.

Updates to assistant tone, hierarchy, or alert behavior could be tried, seen, and refined within the same session — not across a multi-day redesign cycle.

03 Three Product Decisions

Three calls that shaped the workspace.

The brief was open. These are the choices that gave the product its shape.

Decision 01

An assistant, not a form.

Lumen The left pane is a guided FNOL flow that starts with one question — "what kind of incident occurred?" — and unfolds from there.

Agents can switch to Classic mode at any moment. The AI is a co-pilot, never a gatekeeper. The goal was to make the system feel like it's working with the agent, not waiting on them.

Decision 02

A form that knows what it knows.

Sections collapse and expand based on completion. Policy info auto-fills from policy lookup. Each section reports its own state — 3 of 4, Complete, Optional — so agents always know where they are.

Drafts auto-save every keystroke. The claim survives any interruption, because intake never goes smoothly the first time.

Decision 03

Intelligence at the edge of attention.

A right-side panel runs quietly alongside the intake — a live Claim Quality scorecard, severity routing recommendations, and automatic detection of active catastrophe events.

When an incident location falls inside CAT-26-IL-04's storm window, Lumen surfaces it without being asked. The intelligence is there when needed, invisible when not.

04 The Prototype

Not a mockup. A working product.

Built inside Claude with real form behavior, conditional logic, and the Lumen assistant. Stakeholders opened it in a browser and used it — the same way an agent would.

06 Reflection

What this proved to me.

A short list of things I'd carry into the next project.

The takeaway When the goal is alignment, a clickable AI-built prototype can often be more effective than a polished Figma flow. It reduces interpretation gaps because people react to the product itself, not their assumptions about it.

Prototype before polish.

The product vision became real once stakeholders could open it. The screens themselves mattered less than the experience of using them.

AI is a design partner, not just a tool.

Claude pressure-tested ideas, generated working states, and let the design respond to feedback the same hour it arrived.

Alignment beats perfection.

The win wasn't a pristine spec — it was teams arriving at decisions together, faster, on something real.

The medium changes the conversation.

Static review meetings became hands-on walkthroughs. The shape of feedback changed because the artifact did.
